## Working Papers
1. **Do Dairy Digesters Drive Farm Expansion Plans? The Role of Developer Ownership**  
   With Chris Wolf and Wendong Zhang. Pre-Registration: [AEARCTR-0016447](https://www.socialscienceregistry.org/trials/16447)

   <details>
     <summary>Abstract</summary>
     Dairy cattle represent a significant source of methane emissions. Among all abatement technologies, anaerobic digesters are a central climate adaptation tool for the dairy sector. Beyond the substantial variations in state-level incentive policies towards dairy digesters, management of the digester to a third party increases complexity of the adoption decision for dairy farmers. The objective of this paper is to estimate dairy farmer willingness to accept (WTA) new digester installations and how their risk preferences and digester ownership and management arrangements shape their investment decision. Our findings offer timely and relevant guidance for industry and policymakers on the type and magnitude of necessary incentives for farmers in order to stimulate climate adaptation investments in the U.S. dairy sector. We estimate the double-bounded dichotomous contingent valuation responses following the utility-difference approach outlined by Hanemann (1984). WTA estimates differ markedly across ownership models. Farmers require substantially higher compensation under farm ownership than under developer arrangements because they assume the full set of operational responsibilities and risks, including uncertain performance, technology learning, and ongoing management demands. Under a developer-owned model, mean WTA is $364 per cow annually (95% CI: $314–$406), rising slightly to $373 (95% CI: $320–$409) when accounting for risk aversion. In contrast, farm-owned systems require much higher compensation, with mean WTA of $869 and $891 when controlling for risk aversion per cow annually (95% CI: $725–$1,005), reflecting the need to cover added operational exposure and management burden. We convert these WTAs to NPVs to benchmark the scale of incentives needed to trigger installation. Despite contracts that appear financially viable on paper, many respondents were unwilling to accept new digester agreements. Producers cited insufficient returns once time, labor, and management demands were considered.
   </details>

2. **Asymmetric Consumer Responses to Competing Sustainability Attributes: Evidence from a Framed Field Experiment**  
   With Tongzhe Li, Wendong Zhang, and Jingyi Tong. Pre-Registration: [AEARCTR-0016264](https://www.socialscienceregistry.org/trials/16264)

   <details>
     <summary>Abstract</summary>
     We present results of a lab-in-the-field experiment with 913 consumers examining how competing sustainability attributes shape the market viability of produce grown with an innovative production practice. Exposure to negative information about the innovation substantially reduces consumers’ willingness to pay (WTP), while positive information leaves the innovation’s premium unchanged and shifts valuations only by devaluing the product without the innovation. A direct test rejects symmetry of the two information effects, a pattern consistent with reference-dependent preferences under an expectations-based reference point. Balanced information presenting both attributes simultaneously neutralizes the baseline premium. Our experiment provides incentive-compatible field evidence on how consumers process competing environmental information when a product carries environmental benefits and harms, extending evidence on asymmetric information processing to real market choices for a purchasable good under randomized information exposure.
   </details>

3. **Do Dairy Digesters Deliver Promised Methane Reduction? Evidence from Satellite Data**  
   With Lauren Beatty, Wendong Zhang, Mei Mei Collins, and David McLaughlin.

   <details>
     <summary>Abstract</summary>
     We assess whether dairy digesters meaningfully reduce emissions and evaluate the effectiveness of policies that support their deployment. We provide empirical evidence linking digester installation to reductions in methane emissions and atmospheric concentrations using satellite data, which is increasingly popular in agricultural and environmental economics. Exploiting farm-level variation in the timing of digester installation, we estimate the causal effect of digester adoption on methane outcomes using a staggered difference-in-differences design. We demonstrate that the installation of digesters decrease methane emissions using two different satellite data sources measuring methane emissions and concentrations, respectively. First, we use data on “point source detections” of methane emission plumes from the Carbon Mapper Project, which identify emission signatures at spatial scales of tens of meters, enabling attribution at the individual dairy facility. Matching these observed plumes with dairy locations and digester presence information, we estimate that the average treatment effect on the treated on methane emissions is up to -262 kg/hr. This translates into over a 2,000 metric ton annual decrease, which has an associated social cost of $4.5 million.
   </details>

4. **How Belief Updating Turns Consumer Valuation of Low-Methane Milk from “Sounds Yucky” to “Sounds Good”**  
   With Wendong Zhang and Chris Wolf. Pre-Registration: [AEARCTR-0018877](https://www.socialscienceregistry.org/trials/18877/history/309515)
   <details>
     <summary>Abstract</summary>
     Enteric fermentation in the rumen accounts for approximately 50 percent of methane emissions from dairy cattle. Among the most promising intervention strategies are feed additives, including 3-Nitrooxypropanol (3-NOP). Yet novel agricultural technologies frequently meet consumer skepticism as qualitative comments range from "sounds yucky" to "sounds good". It is unclear how the range in scientific estimates of 3-NOP's effectiveness, combined with the spread of misinformation across media platforms and varying levels of trust in information sources, shapes consumers' evaluation of methane-reducing feed additives. To answer these questions, we combine a discrete choice experiment on milk produced with 3-NOP with an elicitation of prior and posterior beliefs, embedded in randomized treatments that vary how scientific evidence on the additive's efficacy is communicated. Preliminary findings suggest that information induces belief convergence toward the communicated value. Consumers with low prior beliefs revise upward, while those with high priors revise downward. The extent of updating varies with consumption patterns, prior knowledge, interest in sustainability, trust in institutions, and media consumption. We further find that believed efficacy increases willingness to pay for low-methane milk. The findings point to strong market potential for low-methane milk, while highlighting that consumer demand is driven by underlying, long-term established beliefs rather than by a sudden information shock.
   </details>

5. **Barriers and Incentives to Anaerobic Digester Adoption: A Nationwide Analysis of U.S. Dairy Farms**  
   With Chris Wolf and Wendong Zhang. *Under review*.

   <details>
     <summary>Abstract</summary>
     Anaerobic digesters (ADs) on U.S. dairy farms reduce methane emissions and generate renewable energy, yet adoption remains low and unevenly distributed. Using 2,976 geo-referenced farm-level observations from 12 states, we find predicted adoption probabilities are highest for farms $>$3,000 cows relative to the smallest farms (200--1,000 cows). Associations between pipeline proximity and predicted adoption vary by farm size, most pronounced among mid-sized operations (2,000--3,000 cows), where a proximity advantage attenuates after 2016, consistent with the nationwide reach of California's Low Carbon Fuel Standard. These patterns suggest size-targeted incentive structures could broaden dairy methane reduction across U.S. farm types.
   </details>
